What Is A Content Management System

18 April 2016 |
InnoCellence Systems Pte. Ltd.

What Is a Content Management System (CMS) and What Can It Do?

In the digital age, corporate websites are often the first points of contact for prospective customers looking for information about brands and their products and/or services. Unfortunately, some companies make bad first impressions by presenting website visitors with outdated design and content.

And poor first impressions can be a costly, especially considering that 83.4% of business-to-business (B2B) buyers research a supplier’s website when making a buying decision, according to Accenture’s 2014 State of B2B Procurement Study.

Fortunately, there’s an easier way to ensure your organization’s web content and design are always up-to-date — with a content management system (CMS).

What Is a Content Management System (CMS)?

A CMS system is a software application or suite of applications designed to handle website functions such as saving, publishing, editing, updating, deleting, and managing the workflow of content.

In short, a CMS system enables you to store and manage all of the visual/textual content shared on your organization’s website.

CMS systems can provide many benefits including:

• Control: You have direct control over the content you want to appear on your website and who you want creating and updating web content.
• Convenience: You can store relational data and publish content without knowing HTML, and revamp your website’s design by installing web templates.
• Scalability: You can add new pages to your website with a few clicks, however, you may still need a programmer’s help for some fine-tuning. • Flexibility: You can expand the functionality of your CMS through plugins that optimize SEO, enable contact forms, conduct user polls, and more.

CMS systems are generally classified into two categories, open and closed source — each with their own set of advantages and disadvantages:

  Advantages Disadvantages
Open Source CMS
<li>Greater technical support since the open source community is active
<li>Usually less costly to adopt and maintain
<li>More customization options from the open source community
<li>Security is an issue as the source code is public
<li>Community created customization options may also pose a security risk
Closed Source CMS
<li>Greater security because the source code isn’t public
<li>Usually requires an annual licensing fee
<li>Higher upfront and maintenance costs
<li>Higher costs for customization

What Types of CMS Systems Are There?

While there are plenty of CMS systems available on the market right now, most share the same basic features — such as editing, publishing, storing, searching for and controlling user access to content.

Of course, what really distinguishes CMS systems from each other is how companies use them to answer the following questions:

1) What kinds of content do you want to share?
2) Which audience(s) do you want to share your content it with?

Every company has different needs and requirements. are some of the most common types of CMS systems available:

CMS Type Purpose Example
Web Content Management System (WCMS) Create, edit, publish, and manage website content on the internet, intranets, and extranets WordPress
Enterprise Content Management System (ECMS) Store and manage large amounts of sensitive company data including employee information and corporate documentation Oracle Webcenter
Component Content Management System (CCMS) Store content components such as images, words, and links for re-use in documentation Vascont
Learning Content Management System (LCMS) Create, store, and manage e-learning content such as video, audio, interactive and gamification apps Joomla LMS
Mobile Content Management System (MCMS) Manage and publish content to mobile devices such as news, games, and SMS messages to both customers and employees Synapsy

Know What a CMS System Can and Can’t Do

Some “out of the box” CMS systems come with so many tools and features that it seems like they can do almost anything — including customer relationship management (CRM), eBooking, and eCommerce.

The reality is that there isn’t a CMS on the market today that comes with full-fledged CRM, eBooking, or eCommerce capabilities.

Yes, it is possible to expand a CMS system’s basic functions by integrating CRM, eCommerce, and eBooking capabilities through plugins, but whether these plugins provide enough functionality to satisfy your bussiness requirements and expectations is another story.

So before you think about adopting a new CMS system, keep this in mind — a CMS system cannot replace a CRM system, but the right one can support eBooking and eCommerce functions.

Does Your Company Need a CMS System?

If your organization is making at least monthly updates to your products and services, need immediate version control over published content, or post content (e.g. blogs, case studies, etc.) regularly — you should consider adopting a CMS system.

However, evaluating the right system is a time consuming process that should always involve the input of you’re your company’s IT specialist or a digital innovation consultant.

After all, choosing a CMS system is long-term commitment that requies a sizeable investment in time (implementation and training) and money (upfront costs and maintenance) — so choose wisely.
